COMPLIANCE RULES
Compliance status, data-quality rules, data-classification handling, and the
regulatory frameworks that apply to the application portfolio

------------------------------------------------------------------------
SCOPE OF THIS DOCUMENT
------------------------------------------------------------------------
This document defines what it means for an application to be compliant, the
data-quality rules that keep the portfolio trustworthy, the handling baseline
for each data-classification level, and the external regulatory frameworks an
architect should reference when advising on an application or a new demand.

It does NOT define the allowed field values or the lifecycle/maturity model
(see portfolio_standards.txt), the governance and technical-architecture rules
(see architecture_policy.txt), or the intake workflow
(see new_application_process.txt).

The key words MUST, SHOULD, and MAY are used in their RFC 2119 sense. This
document is guidance for internal governance; it is not legal advice, and the
authoritative texts are the regulations and standards cited at the end.

------------------------------------------------------------------------
1. COMPLIANCE STATUS MODEL
------------------------------------------------------------------------
compliance_status records the application's standing against the internal
control baseline and applicable regulation. Allowed values:
Compliant, Non-compliant, Not assessed, Unknown.

- Compliant      Assessed and meeting the applicable control baseline.
- Non-compliant  Assessed and found to have one or more control gaps.
- Not assessed   The application is known and in scope, but a compliance review
                 has not yet been performed. This is a legitimate backlog state
                 - the application is queued for assessment - and is distinct
                 from Unknown.
- Unknown        No compliance status has been determined or recorded. This is a
                 DATA-QUALITY DEFECT, not a real state, and MUST be resolved (it
                 means the portfolio cannot tell whether the application has been
                 assessed at all).

The distinction matters: "Not assessed" is a known gap in the schedule;
"Unknown" is a gap in the data. They are remediated differently (Section 3).

------------------------------------------------------------------------
2. CORE COMPLIANCE RULES
------------------------------------------------------------------------
- Every application MUST have a known compliance_status. "Unknown" is treated as
  a data-quality defect and is surfaced for correction.
- "Not assessed" applications MUST have a target assessment date. An application
  that remains "Not assessed" beyond its target date is escalated.
- "Non-compliant" applications MUST have a remediation plan with a named owner
  and a target remediation date.
- Critical applications (business_criticality = Critical) that are
  "Non-compliant" are escalated to the Architecture Review Board.
- Public-facing or AI/ML applications, and any application processing
  Confidential or Restricted data, MUST be Compliant before go-live (i.e. before
  entering Production) and are reviewed more frequently (see Section 5 and
  architecture_policy.txt).

------------------------------------------------------------------------
3. MANDATORY FIELDS AND DATA-QUALITY EXPECTATIONS
------------------------------------------------------------------------
3.1 Mandatory fields
Every application record MUST populate, at minimum:
  app_id, app_name, business_owner, lifecycle_status, business_criticality,
  compliance_status, last_reviewed_date.

A missing or "Unknown" value in any mandatory field is a data-quality anomaly.

3.2 Data-quality anomalies vs statistical outliers
Two different kinds of finding are surfaced and MUST be kept separate:
- Data-quality anomalies are rule-based defects (missing or invalid mandatory
  fields, internal contradictions). They are deterministic and are reported as
  data defects to be corrected.
- Statistical outliers are unusual records identified by anomaly detection over
  the portfolio data (e.g. an unusual combination of attributes). They are
  probabilistic signals for human review, not certain defects.
A record may be both; the two are reported in separate categories so a parsing
or completeness problem is never mistaken for a genuine portfolio insight.

3.3 Rule-based data-quality anomaly catalogue
The following are flagged as data-quality anomalies:
- Missing business_owner (ownership gap; see architecture_policy.txt).
- Missing business_criticality.
- Missing data_classification.
- compliance_status of Unknown.
- Stale review: last_reviewed_date older than the review window
  (see architecture_policy.txt for the 12-month review / 18-month stale
  thresholds).
- Lifecycle/EOL contradiction: end_of_life_date in the past while
  lifecycle_status is Production (the application should be Sunset or Retired).
- Retired-with-footprint: lifecycle_status Retired but with active users,
  non-zero annual cost, or live integrations.
- Criticality/lifecycle contradiction: a Critical application in Sunset or
  Retired (either the criticality rating or the lifecycle state is wrong; see
  architecture_policy.txt).

------------------------------------------------------------------------
4. DATA-CLASSIFICATION HANDLING BASELINE
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Each classification level (values defined in portfolio_standards.txt) imposes a
minimum handling baseline. Higher levels inherit the controls of lower levels.

- Public
  No confidentiality controls required for the data itself; integrity and
  availability controls still apply. Publication still follows the normal
  approval process.
- Internal (default)
  Access limited to authenticated internal users on a need-to-know basis.
  Encryption in transit. Standard access logging.
- Confidential
  Access on least-privilege, role-based grants with periodic recertification.
  Encryption in transit AND at rest. Audit logging of access. If personal data
  is involved, the data-protection rules in Section 5.3 apply.
- Restricted
  As Confidential, plus: strong/multi-factor authentication for access; strict
  segregation and minimised access; enhanced monitoring; explicit approval for
  any export, copy, or third-party/cross-border transfer. Restricted data in
  public-cloud or SaaS hosting requires explicit risk acceptance and an assessed,
  Compliant status before go-live.

Hosting model (portfolio_standards.txt) and these handling baselines are read
together: e.g. Restricted data on Public Cloud or SaaS raises the bar for
encryption, key management, and data-residency evidence.

------------------------------------------------------------------------
5. REGULATORY AND STANDARDS LANDSCAPE
------------------------------------------------------------------------
This section gives the architect (and the domain agent) authoritative reference
points for advising on an application or a new demand. For each framework: what
it is, when it applies, and what to check. Always confirm current applicability
against the source texts cited in "Sources / further reading".

5.1 EU NIS2 Directive and ENISA guidance (cybersecurity risk management)
- What: The NIS2 Directive (EU) 2022/2555 raises cybersecurity risk-management
  and incident-reporting requirements across 18 critical sectors, including
  energy. Article 21(2) lists the baseline measures (risk analysis, incident
  handling, business continuity, supply-chain security, access control,
  cryptography, etc.). Commission Implementing Regulation (EU) 2024/2690
  (17 Oct 2024) sets technical and methodological requirements for certain
  entity types, and ENISA's Technical Implementation Guidance (v1.0, 26 Jun 2025)
  translates these into practical measures and evidence examples and maps them
  to ISO/IEC and other standards.
- When it applies: Primarily to EU essential/important entities. For a Swiss
  group it is most relevant for EU subsidiaries/operations and as a de-facto
  baseline; the binding Swiss obligations are in Section 5.2.
- What to check: For in-scope applications, whether the Article 21(2) control
  areas are addressed (access control, cryptography, logging/monitoring,
  business continuity, supply-chain/vendor security) and whether incident
  handling and reporting are in place.

5.2 Switzerland - Information Security Act (ISA) and the energy ICT minimum
    standard (binding for critical infrastructure)
- What: Since 1 April 2025, operators of critical infrastructure in Switzerland
  (including energy) MUST report significant cyberattacks to the National Cyber
  Security Centre (NCSC) within 24 hours of discovery, with a complete follow-up
  report within 14 days. Sanctions for failure to report apply from 1 October
  2025. Separately, the ICT minimum standard for electricity grid operators has
  been mandatory since 1 July 2024 (via the revised Electricity Supply
  Ordinance), and a comparable obligation applies to gas from 1 July 2025.
- When it applies: To applications and systems supporting Swiss critical
  infrastructure operations - directly relevant to Energy Trading, Grid
  Operations, and Asset Performance applications in this portfolio.
- What to check: For energy/grid-supporting applications, whether they fall
  under the ICT minimum standard and whether incident detection and the 24-hour
  NCSC reporting path are in place. Note overlapping reporting duties: a single
  incident may also require notification to FINMA (if financial supervision
  applies) and to the Federal Data Protection and Information Commissioner
  (FDPIC) where personal data is breached.

5.3 Data protection - EU GDPR and the Swiss Federal Act on Data Protection (FADP)
- What: The GDPR (EU) and the revised Swiss FADP (in force since 1 Sep 2023)
  govern processing of personal data: lawful basis, data minimisation, purpose
  limitation, data-subject rights, records of processing, and breach
  notification.
- When it applies: Whenever an application processes personal data of EU or
  Swiss individuals (employees, customers, counterparties).
- What to check: Whether personal data is in scope (drives Confidential
  classification at least), whether a record of processing and a lawful basis
  exist, whether a data-protection impact assessment is needed for high-risk or
  AI/profiling use, and whether breach-notification paths exist (FDPIC for Swiss,
  the relevant supervisory authority for EU).

5.4 Security control baselines - ISO/IEC 27001 and NIST CSF
- What: ISO/IEC 27001:2022 (with Annex A controls from ISO/IEC 27002:2022) is
  the certifiable information-security management standard; the NIST Cybersecurity
  Framework 2.0 (2024) provides an outcome-based structure (Govern, Identify,
  Protect, Detect, Respond, Recover).
- When it applies: As the internal control baseline against which "Compliant"
  is judged, and as the mapping target for NIS2/Swiss requirements.
- What to check: Whether the application's controls map to the organisation's
  ISO 27001 / NIST CSF baseline appropriate to its criticality and data
  classification.

------------------------------------------------------------------------
6. APPLYING THIS IN A NEW-DEMAND CONVERSATION
------------------------------------------------------------------------
When an architect is reasoning about a new or proposed application, a useful
order of questions is:
1. What data does it process? -> set the data_classification and the handling
   baseline (Section 4). Personal data -> GDPR/FADP (5.3).
2. Does it support critical infrastructure (energy, grid)? -> Swiss ICT minimum
   standard and 24-hour NCSC reporting (5.2); NIS2/ENISA baseline (5.1).
3. Is it public-facing or AI/ML, or does it process Confidential/Restricted
   data? -> elevated governance review and "Compliant before go-live"
   (Section 2; architecture_policy.txt).
4. Which controls must it meet? -> map to the ISO 27001 / NIST CSF baseline
   (5.4) at the level its criticality and classification demand.
5. What is its compliance_status and review cadence? -> ensure it is not
   "Unknown", has a remediation plan if "Non-compliant", and a review schedule
   appropriate to its risk.
This sequence lets the portfolio give specific, source-backed guidance rather
than generic advice, and keeps the answer tied to the application's actual
classification, criticality, and sector.
