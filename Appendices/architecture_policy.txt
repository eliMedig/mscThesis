ARCHITECTURE POLICY
Enterprise architecture governance policy and technical standards for the
application portfolio

------------------------------------------------------------------------
SCOPE OF THIS DOCUMENT
------------------------------------------------------------------------
This document defines the RULES that govern applications and the technical
standards their design must meet: ownership and accountability, business
criticality and resilience, lifecycle/investment alignment, review cadence,
governance bodies, integration standards, authentication and authorisation,
API security, documentation, and the additional governance for AI and
public-facing applications.

It does NOT define the allowed field values or the lifecycle/maturity model
(see portfolio_standards.txt), the compliance status, data-quality rules, or
data-classification handling baselines and regulatory frameworks (see
compliance_rules.txt), or the intake workflow (see new_application_process.txt).

The key words MUST, SHOULD, and MAY are used in their RFC 2119 sense.

------------------------------------------------------------------------
1. PURPOSE
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Govern the application portfolio so that investment, risk, and technical debt
stay aligned with the target architecture, and so that applications are owned,
secure, integrable, and operable. The policy applies to all applications in the
portfolio and to all changes routed through the intake process.

------------------------------------------------------------------------
2. ARCHITECTURE PRINCIPLES
------------------------------------------------------------------------
- Reuse before buy before build. Before approving a new application, reuse of an
  existing capability is considered first, then commercial (COTS/SaaS) options,
  then bespoke build. The sourcing taxonomy is in portfolio_standards.txt; the
  duplication check is in new_application_process.txt.
- Align to the target architecture. New and changed applications SHOULD move the
  portfolio toward the reference technology stack and integration model, not
  away from it.
- Manage technical debt deliberately. Low-maturity applications (maturity 1-2,
  per portfolio_standards.txt) in Production MUST have a modernisation or
  retirement plan; debt is made visible, not hidden.
- Secure and private by design. Security and data-protection controls are
  designed in from intake, proportionate to criticality and data classification,
  not added afterward.
- Human accountability for automated decisions. Where an application automates
  or materially influences a decision (including AI/ML), a named human remains
  accountable for the outcome.
- Decisions are documented. Significant architecture decisions are recorded
  (Section 13) so they can be reviewed and revisited.

------------------------------------------------------------------------
3. OWNERSHIP AND ACCOUNTABILITY
------------------------------------------------------------------------
- Every application MUST have a named accountable owner (a person or role) -
  the business_owner. An application_manager MAY also be recorded for
  operational responsibility.
- Applications without an owner are non-conformant and MUST be remediated within
  one review cycle. A missing owner is also a data-quality anomaly
  (compliance_rules.txt).
- The owner is accountable for the application's data classification,
  compliance status, lifecycle decisions, and the accuracy of its portfolio
  record.

------------------------------------------------------------------------
4. BUSINESS CRITICALITY AND RESILIENCE
------------------------------------------------------------------------
- Each application is rated Low / Medium / High / Critical (definitions in
  portfolio_standards.txt).
- Critical applications MUST have documented recovery objectives (a recovery
  time objective, RTO, and a recovery point objective, RPO) and MUST undergo an
  annual resilience review (e.g. backup/restore validation, failover or
  continuity testing).
- High applications SHOULD have documented recovery objectives and a periodic
  resilience review.
- Recovery objectives MUST be consistent with the application's role: a Critical
  application supporting trading or grid operations carries the tightest
  objectives and the strongest continuity expectations.

------------------------------------------------------------------------
5. LIFECYCLE AND INVESTMENT ALIGNMENT
------------------------------------------------------------------------
(Lifecycle states are defined in portfolio_standards.txt: Plan, Build,
Production, Sunset, Retired.)
- Applications in Sunset or Retired MUST NOT receive new feature investment;
  only run, decommissioning, or data-migration work is permitted.
- A Critical application in Sunset or Retired is a governance contradiction and
  MUST be escalated: either the criticality rating or the lifecycle state is
  wrong. (This contradiction is surfaced as a data-quality anomaly in
  compliance_rules.txt.)
- Investment SHOULD follow portfolio segmentation (the TIME model in
  portfolio_standards.txt): Invest on high-value/high-fitness applications,
  Migrate (modernise) high-value/low-fitness ones, Tolerate low-value/high-
  fitness ones, and Eliminate low-value/low-fitness ones.

------------------------------------------------------------------------
6. REVIEW CADENCE
------------------------------------------------------------------------
- Every application MUST be reviewed at least every 12 months; last_reviewed_date
  is updated at each review.
- An application not reviewed within 18 months is treated as stale and is flagged
  for governance attention (a data-quality anomaly in compliance_rules.txt).
- Higher-risk applications are reviewed more frequently: public-facing or AI/ML
  applications, and any application processing Confidential or Restricted data,
  are reviewed at least every 6 months (Section 11).

------------------------------------------------------------------------
7. GOVERNANCE BODIES AND DECISION RIGHTS
------------------------------------------------------------------------
- Domain Architect: reviews and approves Demands within their domain
  (new_application_process.txt), maintains domain standards, and assesses change
  and environmental impact.
- Architecture Review Board (ARB): decides cross-domain matters, standard
  exceptions/waivers (Section 14), and elevated-risk Demands (Critical
  criticality, public-facing, AI/ML, Confidential/Restricted data, or
  Non-compliant Critical applications).
- Decisions of the Domain Architect and ARB are recorded against the affected
  application(s) and as architecture decision records where they set or change a
  standard (Section 13).

------------------------------------------------------------------------
8. INTEGRATION STANDARDS
------------------------------------------------------------------------
8.1 Integration via the central integration layer
- Integrations SHOULD be realised through the central integration layer
  (integration platform / iPaaS / managed APIs), not as ad-hoc point-to-point
  links. The integration layer provides mediation, observability, and a managed
  contract between systems.
- Direct point-to-point integration, and especially direct database-to-database
  coupling between applications, is discouraged and requires explicit
  justification and architect approval (it creates hidden coupling and raises
  change risk).

8.2 Approved integration patterns (choose the lightest that fits)
- Synchronous request/response (API): for real-time queries and commands where
  the caller needs an immediate answer. Realised as HTTPS REST APIs (or an
  established standard such as GraphQL/gRPC where justified).
- Asynchronous events/messaging: for decoupled, event-driven flows and for
  propagating state changes. Preferred where systems should not be tightly
  coupled in time.
- Batch / file transfer: for high-volume or scheduled bulk data movement, over a
  secure channel (e.g. SFTP). Used where real-time or event flow is unnecessary.
Established enterprise integration patterns (Hohpe & Woolf) and event-driven
design inform these choices.

8.3 Direction, system of record, and contracts
- For every integration, the system of record for the data and the direction of
  flow MUST be defined (which system owns the data, which consumes it).
- Interfaces MUST have an explicit, versioned contract (e.g. an OpenAPI
  specification for REST, an event schema for messaging). Breaking changes
  require versioning and a managed consumer migration; producers do not break
  consumers silently.
- Integrations are recorded against the application (integration_count in
  portfolio_standards.txt) and in the interface catalogue (Section 13).

------------------------------------------------------------------------
9. AUTHENTICATION AND AUTHORISATION
------------------------------------------------------------------------
9.1 Identity and single sign-on (human users)
- Applications MUST authenticate human users through the enterprise identity
  provider using the approved SSO pattern: SAML 2.0 or OpenID Connect (OIDC).
  Local, application-specific user stores and passwords are avoided.
- Multi-factor authentication (MFA) MUST be enforced for administrative access,
  for remote access, and for any access to applications processing Confidential
  or Restricted data.
- Authorisation MUST follow least privilege using role-based access control
  (RBAC), with attribute-based access control (ABAC) where finer-grained,
  context-aware decisions are needed. Access to Confidential/Restricted data is
  recertified periodically.

9.2 API and service-to-service authentication/authorisation
- Service and API authorisation MUST use OAuth 2.0 (with OpenID Connect for
  identity) issuing short-lived, scoped access tokens. Token scopes are
  least-privilege and validated on every request (including token expiry and
  audience).
- Service-to-service calls SHOULD use mutual TLS (mTLS) and/or signed tokens for
  workload identity, consistent with a zero-trust posture (no implicit trust
  based on network location).
- Static API keys MAY be used only for low-risk, non-sensitive machine access;
  where used they MUST be unique per consumer, transmitted over TLS, rotatable,
  revocable, and never embedded in client-side code or URLs. They are not a
  substitute for OAuth where sensitive data or actions are involved.
- Authorisation is enforced server-side on every object and every function
  (see API security, Section 10) - never assumed from the UI or the caller's
  identity alone.

9.3 Credentials and secrets
- Secrets (keys, tokens, certificates, connection strings) MUST be stored in a
  managed secrets store and MUST NOT be hard-coded in source, configuration, or
  client code. Credentials and certificates are rotated, and access to secrets
  is logged.

Identity assurance and authenticator strength follow recognised guidance
(NIST SP 800-63); the overall model follows zero-trust principles
(NIST SP 800-207).

------------------------------------------------------------------------
10. API SECURITY
------------------------------------------------------------------------
APIs (especially internet-exposed ones) MUST be designed against the OWASP API
Security Top 10 (2023). In particular:
- Enforce object-level and function-level authorisation on every request
  (guarding against BOLA - API1, and BFLA - API5).
- Use strong, correctly implemented authentication; validate token expiry and
  audience (guarding against Broken Authentication - API2).
- Return and accept only the properties each consumer is entitled to; avoid
  mass-assignment and over-exposure (Broken Object Property Level Authorization
  - API3).
- Apply rate limiting, quotas, and payload/size limits at the API gateway
  (Unrestricted Resource Consumption - API4).
- Protect sensitive business flows from automated abuse (API6).
- Validate and constrain outbound requests and never blindly fetch
  user-supplied URLs (SSRF - API7).
- Harden configuration and disable unused features/verbose errors (Security
  Misconfiguration - API8).
- Maintain an inventory of APIs, versions, and environments; retire and
  document deprecated endpoints (Improper Inventory Management - API9).
- Validate and constrain data consumed from third-party/upstream APIs (Unsafe
  Consumption of APIs - API10).
Externally exposed APIs SHOULD be fronted by an API gateway providing
authentication, rate limiting, logging, and schema validation. Sensitive data
MUST NOT be placed in URLs/query strings.

------------------------------------------------------------------------
11. AI/ML AND PUBLIC-FACING APPLICATIONS (ADDITIONAL GOVERNANCE)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Applications that use AI/ML, or are public-facing (internet-exposed), require an
additional governance review at intake and on an ongoing basis:
- A data-protection assessment (and a formal data-protection impact assessment
  where high-risk processing or profiling is involved; see compliance_rules.txt
  for GDPR/FADP).
- A recorded data classification for the data the application processes and, for
  AI/ML, for training and inference data.
- A named accountable human owner for decisions the AI influences or makes
  (Section 2; the human-in-the-loop principle).
- For AI/ML: documentation of the model's purpose and limitations, the data
  sources used, and how outputs are reviewed; monitoring for drift and for
  inappropriate or biased outputs.
- Public-facing or AI-integrated applications that handle Confidential data MUST
  be Compliant before go-live (before entering Production) and are reviewed at
  least every 6 months (Section 6; compliance_rules.txt).

------------------------------------------------------------------------
12. CLOUD AND HOSTING ALIGNMENT
------------------------------------------------------------------------
- Cloud-hosted applications (Public Cloud (AWS) / Public Cloud (Azure) / Private
  Cloud / SaaS, per portfolio_standards.txt) MUST deploy into an approved landing
  zone / tenancy with baseline security controls (identity integration, logging,
  network segmentation, encryption).
- Encryption in transit is mandatory for all applications; encryption at rest is
  mandatory for Confidential and Restricted data (handling baselines in
  compliance_rules.txt). Key management uses the approved key-management service.
- Data residency and cross-border transfer constraints (compliance_rules.txt)
  MUST be satisfied by the chosen hosting model and region.

------------------------------------------------------------------------
13. DOCUMENTATION REQUIREMENTS
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Every application MUST maintain current documentation proportionate to its
criticality:
- Architecture decision records (ADRs): significant decisions (sourcing,
  integration pattern, hosting, authentication model, standard exceptions) are
  recorded as short, dated ADRs capturing context, decision, and consequences.
- A solution/context overview: a high-level architecture description (the C4
  model and/or arc42 are recommended structures) showing the application, its
  users, and its system context.
- Data-flow documentation: what data the application holds and exchanges, its
  classification, and with which systems/parties it flows (supports compliance
  and integration review).
- Interface/integration catalogue: each integration's pattern, direction,
  contract/version, and system of record (Section 8).
- Operational documentation: runbooks for operation and recovery, consistent
  with the resilience obligations in Section 4 (especially for High/Critical).
Documentation MUST be kept current; a Production application with materially
outdated or missing required documentation is flagged at review.

------------------------------------------------------------------------
14. EXCEPTIONS AND WAIVERS
------------------------------------------------------------------------
- Any deviation from this policy or an architecture standard requires a recorded
  waiver, approved by the ARB, with a stated rationale, a risk owner, compensating
  controls, and an expiry date.
- Waivers are time-boxed and reviewed at expiry; an expired waiver returns the
  application to non-conformant status.
- A standing register of active waivers is maintained and reviewed by the ARB.
